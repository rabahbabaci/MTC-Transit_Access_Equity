Transit Access Equity in Alameda County

Time-Aware Isochrones & Job Accessibility Using R5

CYPLAN 101 — Track B (Advanced) | Fall 2025

Authors: Rabah Babaci & Adithya Ayanam

Introduction

Transit access is not constant throughout the day. While the AM Peak period benefits from high frequency and coordinated transfers, late-night service often operates with long headways and reduced coverage. For workers who rely on transit—especially those living in Equity Priority Communities (EPCs)—these variations can meaningfully change the number of reachable jobs and opportunities.

This project investigates a central question: How does transit-based job accessibility change from the AM Peak (7:30–8:30 AM) to Late Night (10:00–11:00 PM), and do EPC tracts experience disproportionately larger losses?

Using the R5 routing engine, we compute detailed travel-time matrices, isochrones, and job-reachability metrics for six representative tracts across Alameda County.

Methods

Data Sources

We integrate multiple datasets to construct a multimodal routing and accessibility framework:

  • TIGER/Line tract boundaries (2022)
  • ACS 2018–2022 population
  • LEHD LODES 2021 workplace data
  • MTC Plan Bay Area 2050 EPC classification
  • GTFS for AC Transit and BART
  • OpenStreetMap street network for Alameda County

All GEOIDs were standardized, geometries validated, and dark/missing data were systematically documented following course guidelines.

R5 Routing

R5 was used to compute complete travel-time matrices for two periods:

  • AM Peak (7:30–8:30 AM)
  • Late Night (10:00–11:00 PM)

Six origin tracts (3 EPC, 3 non-EPC) were selected across Oakland, San Leandro, Fremont/Union City, and Hayward. Isochrones were generated for 15/30/45 minutes, and job accessibility metrics were linked using LODES workplace counts.

Limitations

Several data and methodological constraints shape this analysis:

  • GTFS represents scheduled, not real, service. Reliability issues or cancellations are not captured.
  • LODES workplace data includes imputed and suppressed values.
  • ACS estimates include sampling variability, especially for small-population tracts.
  • Ecological fallacy risk: tract-level summaries do not represent individual experiences.
  • MAUP: tract boundaries influence spatial patterns.
  • Isochrones are convex hulls—they may overgeneralize reach in complex networks.

These issues highlight the need to interpret the results as indicative patterns rather than precise operational performance measures.

Policy Considerations

The findings suggest several actionable points for transit planning:

  • EPC tracts in Oakland and Hayward experience the largest nighttime losses and could benefit from stronger span-of-service improvements.
  • Late-night headway consolidation on key corridors could reduce transfer friction.
  • Southern Alameda County, while not severely impacted, still faces long base travel times.

Equity-focused service planning should consider both frequency and reliability across the full day—not only during the peak period.
