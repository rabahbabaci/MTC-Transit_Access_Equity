1. Introduction and Motivation

The San Francisco Bay Area is recognized for its diverse communities and complex transportation networks, yet many residentsâ€”particularly those living in Equity Priority Communities (EPCs)â€”face barriers to accessing jobs, healthcare, and essential services through public transit. Traditional transportation studies often measure accessibility in static ways, assuming that travel times remain constant throughout the day. In reality, transit frequency, congestion, and transfer availability change significantly between morning, midday, evening, and late-night hours. These temporal differences can create hidden inequities that are not visible in conventional analyses.

This project, developed under the mentorship of the Metropolitan Transportation Commission (MTC), investigates how transit accessibility varies across time and geography within Alameda County. The overarching goal is to provide time-aware metrics that can help planners and policymakers design more equitable service schedules, reduce off-peak isolation, and improve regional mobility for vulnerable populations.

2. Objectives and Research Questions

The primary objective of this project is to evaluate transit accessibility in a way that reflects both spatial and temporal equity. Specifically, the project aims to quantify how many jobs are reachable within specific travel-time thresholdsâ€”15, 30, and 45 minutesâ€”and to compare these metrics between EPC and non-EPC tracts in Alameda County.

Additional objectives include developing a reproducible workflow for processing and analyzing large geospatial datasets, creating interactive and animated visualizations, and hosting a public storytelling webpage that communicates findings clearly.

The key research questions guiding this study are:

  • How does transit accessibility differ by time of day (morning, midday, evening, and late night)?
  • Which census tracts and demographic groups, particularly EPCs, experience the greatest reduction in access during off-peak hours?
  • How can time-aware indicators support data-driven decisions about equitable transit service planning?

3. Data Sources and Description

To explore these questions, we combined multiple datasets that describe both the social and spatial characteristics of Alameda County. The MTC Plan Bay Area 2050 EPC Layer identifies census tracts that qualify as Equity Priority Communities based on income, race, vehicle access, and linguistic isolation criteria. The U.S. Census TIGER/Line 2022 Tract Shapefiles provide the geographic boundaries necessary for spatial analysis. Socioeconomic indicators such as population, income, and education were obtained from the American Community Survey (ACS 2018â€“2022 5-Year Estimates) using the Census API.

Employment data came from the LEHD LODES Workplace Area Characteristics dataset, which lists the number of jobs by tract. In future phases, General Transit Feed Specification (GTFS) feeds from 511 SF Bay and OpenStreetMap (OSM) street networks will be integrated to construct time-aware transport models. All data were standardized to the WGS 84 coordinate system (EPSG:4326) and stored in the projectâ€™s /data/raw and /data/processed directories for reproducibility.

4. Methods and Workflow

4.1 Data Cleaning and Integration

The cleaning and integration phase established a unified dataset suitable for accessibility analysis. First, the statewide TIGER/Line Tract Shapefile was filtered to include only Alameda County (COUNTYFP = 001). Each tractâ€™s 11-digit GEOID was standardized to ensure consistency across datasets. The MTC EPC Layer was then joined to the tract geometry using this key, and an EPC flag (is_epc = 1) was created for tracts classified as High, Higher, or Highest priority.

Next, demographic attributes such as total population and median income were merged from the ACS dataset. Missing population values were flagged and filled with zero to preserve full tract coverage. Finally, all geometries were reprojected to EPSG 4326 for mapping and web visualization. The cleaned GeoDataFrame contained 379 tracts, 97 of which were EPCs and 282 non-EPCs. Validation confirmed that every geometry was valid and that there were no missing identifiers.

4.2 Preliminary Accessibility Proxy

Because time-specific GTFS data have not yet been integrated, a preliminary accessibility metric was calculated as a proxy. The centroids of each tract were used to estimate potential job reachability within a 30-minute travel time window, assuming an average effective speed of 40 km/h. This approach provided a first-order estimate of spatial access differences across the county.

Employment densities were computed from the LODES Workplace Area Characteristics data and aggregated by tract. The number of jobs reachable within the 30-minute threshold was then compared between EPC and non-EPC tracts. While simplified, this proxy produced meaningful insights into the spatial structure of opportunity and will later be replaced by dynamic, time-aware isochrones generated from R5py network models.

4.3 Visualization and Reproducibility

A major emphasis of the project is on transparent, reproducible analysis. All scripts are organized within /notebooks and /src directories, and output files are automatically exported to /data/processed, /visualizations, and /results.

For the midterm phase, multiple visualization methods were used:

  • Interactive Folium Map: Displays Alameda County tracts, highlighting EPCs in red and non-EPCs in blue. Hover tooltips show GEOID, population, and EPC status.
  • Choropleth Maps: Illustrate spatial variations in jobs reachable within 30 minutes.
  • Boxplots and Bar Charts: Compare accessibility distributions between EPC and non-EPC tracts.

Each visualization was exported as either static PNG images or interactive HTML files to support embedding on the public GitHub Pages site. This ensures that others can rerun the notebook and reproduce identical results.

5. Results and Findings

The preliminary analysis revealed several important findings. On average, Equity Priority Community (EPC) tracts could reach approximately 75,000 more jobs within 30 minutes than non-EPC tracts, representing roughly a 22 percent higher accessibility level. These tracts are concentrated along the western corridor of Alameda Countyâ€” spanning Berkeley, Oakland, and Haywardâ€”where transit density and job opportunities are highest.

In contrast, eastern tracts near Livermore and Pleasanton show significantly lower accessibility because of both reduced transit coverage and greater spatial separation from employment centers. The boxplot and bar chart visualizations confirm a consistent accessibility gap between EPC and non-EPC groups. All datasets passed quality validation: there were no missing geometries, and population and EPC flag values were properly merged.

6. Discussion

The results suggest that while EPCs are often located closer to job centers, they still face challenges related to off-peak transit frequency and schedule alignment. The current analysis, based on a static travel speed, captures spatial patterns but not temporal variation. Once real GTFS data are integrated in the final phase, we expect to observe significant reductions in off-peak job access, particularly in EPC tracts that depend on infrequent bus routes.

The work also highlights the value of open data and reproducible workflows in regional planning. Every stepâ€”from API data collection to visualizationâ€”is scripted, enabling other students or agencies to replicate or extend the analysis.

7. Limitations and Future Work

Several limitations remain at the midterm stage. The current model assumes a constant average travel speed of 40 km/h, which simplifies real-world transit variability. The accessibility metric is distance-based and does not yet account for walking, waiting, or transfer times. Additionally, the analysis is limited to Alameda County, although the data architecture supports regional scaling.

Future work will expand on these foundations by:

  • Incorporating 511 GTFS feeds and building a multimodal R5py network model.
  • Generating 15-, 30-, and 45-minute isochrones for four time periods (AM, Midday, PM, Late Night).
  • Visualizing accessibility change through Plotly time-sliders or Kepler.gl animations.
  • Interpreting results within a policy framework to propose transit schedule adjustments and equity-focused investments.

8. Conclusion

This midterm phase successfully built a clean, validated, and fully reproducible dataset for Alameda County and demonstrated clear accessibility differences between EPC and non-EPC tracts. The integration of open data sources, transparent coding practices, and visual storytelling establishes a strong technical foundation for the upcoming time-aware accessibility analysis.

By advancing from static to dynamic measures of transit equity, this work supports Plan Bay Area 2050 goals of fostering inclusive, sustainable, and data-driven transportation planning across the region.
