1. Introduction and Motivation

The San Francisco Bay Area is recognized for its diverse communities and complex transportation networks, yet many residentsâ€”particularly those living in Equity Priority Communities (EPCs)â€”face barriers to accessing jobs, healthcare, and essential services through public transit. Traditional transportation studies often measure accessibility in static ways, assuming that travel times remain constant throughout the day. In reality, transit frequency, congestion, and transfer availability change significantly between morning, midday, evening, and late-night hours. These temporal differences can create hidden inequities that are not visible in conventional analyses.

This project, developed under the mentorship of the Metropolitan Transportation Commission (MTC), investigates how transit accessibility varies across time and geography within Alameda County. The overarching goal is to provide time-aware metrics that can help planners and policymakers design more equitable service schedules, reduce off-peak isolation, and improve regional mobility for vulnerable populations.

5. Results and Findings

The preliminary analysis revealed several important findings. On average, Equity Priority Community (EPC) tracts could reach approximately 75,000 more jobs within 30 minutes than non-EPC tracts, representing roughly a 22 percent higher accessibility level. These tracts are concentrated along the western corridor of Alameda Countyâ€” spanning Berkeley, Oakland, and Haywardâ€”where transit density and job opportunities are highest.

In contrast, eastern tracts near Livermore and Pleasanton show significantly lower accessibility because of both reduced transit coverage and greater spatial separation from employment centers. The boxplot and bar chart visualizations confirm a consistent accessibility gap between EPC and non-EPC groups. All datasets passed quality validation: there were no missing geometries, and population and EPC flag values were properly merged.

6. Discussion

The results suggest that while EPCs are often located closer to job centers, they still face challenges related to off-peak transit frequency and schedule alignment. The current analysis, based on a static travel speed, captures spatial patterns but not temporal variation. Once real GTFS data are integrated in the final phase, we expect to observe significant reductions in off-peak job access, particularly in EPC tracts that depend on infrequent bus routes.

The work also highlights the value of open data and reproducible workflows in regional planning. Every stepâ€”from API data collection to visualizationâ€”is scripted, enabling other students or agencies to replicate or extend the analysis.

7. Limitations and Future Work

Several limitations remain at the midterm stage. The current model assumes a constant average travel speed of 40 km/h, which simplifies real-world transit variability. The accessibility metric is distance-based and does not yet account for walking, waiting, or transfer times. Additionally, the analysis is limited to Alameda County, although the data architecture supports regional scaling.

Future work will expand on these foundations by:

  • Incorporating 511 GTFS feeds and building a multimodal R5py network model.
  • Generating 15-, 30-, and 45-minute isochrones for four time periods (AM, Midday, PM, Late Night).
  • Visualizing accessibility change through Plotly time-sliders or Kepler.gl animations.
  • Interpreting results within a policy framework to propose transit schedule adjustments and equity-focused investments.

8. Conclusion

This midterm phase successfully built a clean, validated, and fully reproducible dataset for Alameda County and demonstrated clear accessibility differences between EPC and non-EPC tracts. The integration of open data sources, transparent coding practices, and visual storytelling establishes a strong technical foundation for the upcoming time-aware accessibility analysis.

By advancing from static to dynamic measures of transit equity, this work supports Plan Bay Area 2050 goals of fostering inclusive, sustainable, and data-driven transportation planning across the region.
